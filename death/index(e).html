What is Death Metal?

Death metal is an extreme subgenre of heavy metal. The specific traits of death metal generally trace back to the mid-1980s from the 1970s heavy metal. It is characterized by having rhythmic elements from biker metal, when several bands began to incorporate the aggressive hard sound of thrash metal. Death metal developed strongly toward the very specific, dark rhythm of extreme metal, with the raw sound of hardcore punk. This genre is much heavier compared to its relative, thrash metal.

Characteristics

It is characterized by the use of guttural vocals, guitars with excessive overdrive, low tunings for instruments, fast percussion (with a dominant use of double pedals), complex musical structures, as well as unorthodox techniques for composing guitar riffs. The themes of death metal cover topics like horror stories, religion, philosophy, serial killers, cannibalism, torture, and others. On the other hand, it is influenced by the physical and moral problems of man: death, militarism, society, enlightenment, philosophy, and others.

Timeline and Impact

Death metal was born in the 80s, evolving from thrash metal with a more extreme and brutal sound. Possessed released *Seven Churches* (1985), but it was Death with *Scream Bloody Gore* (1987) who consolidated the genre. In the 90s, death metal expanded with scenes in the U.S. (Morbid Angel, Cannibal Corpse, Obituary) and Sweden (Entombed, Dismember). Over time, subgenres like technical death metal, melodic death metal, and brutal death metal emerged.
